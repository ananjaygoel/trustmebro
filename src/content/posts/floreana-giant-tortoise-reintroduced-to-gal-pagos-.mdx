## The Details

Photograph: Carlos Espinosa /PR Galápagos Islands Floreana giant tortoise reintroduced to Galápagos island after almost 200 years Subspecies driven to extinction returns after ‘back breeding’ programme using partial descendants Patrick Barkham Fri 20 Feb 2026 16. 00 EST Prefer the Guardian on Google Giant tortoises, the life-giving engineers of remote small island ecosystems, are plodding over the Galápagos island of Floreana for the first time in more than 180 years.

Remarkably, 158 juvenile giant tortoises descended from the Floreana subspecies have been returned to the island in a vital step for the largest ecological restoration project undertaken on the Pacific Ocean archipelago. A relic population of giant tortoises found out on Wolf volcano in the north of Isabela Island in 2008 were found to be descended in part from the Floreana population .

## Why This Matters

Most of the Wolf volcano tortoises had domed shells like those living on Isabela’s other volcanoes to the south, but some had a saddleback-shaped carapace more typical of the tortoises that evolved on Floreana. A captive “back breeding” programme began in 2017, for which 23 hybrid tortoises most closely related to the Floreana subspecies were chosen to recreate it as genetically close to the original as possible. More than 600 hatchlings had been born by 2025, and several hundred grew large enough to be returned to their ancestral island.

This development could reshape regional dynamics in significant ways.
