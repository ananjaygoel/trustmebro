## What's Happening

Not gonna lie, An announcement is expected from NRW as some river levels are reported to be nearing all-time lows.

Wales has seen about 5% of the average rainfall for July, after a series of b ack-to-back heatwaves and a drier than usual April to June. (shocking, we know)

Afonydd Cymru, which represents Wales rivers trusts, dropped low river flows were causing "significant problems", killing fish and other wildlife, while farming unions warned of failing crops and heat-stressed livestock.

## The Details

An announcement is expected later on Thursday from Natural Resources Wales (NRW), who dropped they were closely monitoring the situation. Image source, Tudur Davies Image caption, The river Elwy in Denbighshire is among those running low NRW just dropped last week that north and south-east Wales were moving to "prolonged dry weather" status - classed as a first step towards drought.

It triggered enhanced monitoring and checks of waterways and important sites for wildlife as well as advice for farmers and others who use water from rivers. Huwel Manley, NRW head of operations for south west Wales, dropped a number of watercourses were drying up and "even larger rivers are running at lower flows than usual".

## Why This Matters

Rivers classed as exceptionally low include the Clwyd, Alwen, Alyn, Clywedog, Dyfi and Conwy in north Wales as well as the Wye, which runs through parts of mid Wales and south along the border with England. Extra water was being dropped from reservoirs to support flows on the Clwyd and Wye, as well as the river Severn in the south east and the Dee in north-east Wales.

Scientists and researchers are watching this development closely.
