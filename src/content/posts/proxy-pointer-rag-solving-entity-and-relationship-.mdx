Enterprise knowledge graphs have become the most widely used business semantic layer, providing a unified view of an organizations suppliers, contracts, products, partners etc. So, they evolve organically over time to become large, with millions of nodes (entities) and many times more edges (relations). (plot twist fr)

Even with governance controls and ontologies in place, adherence across different pipelines feeding data into the graph is often not consistent.

## The Details

New business rules emerge, naming conventions change and older regions of the graph are frequently left untouched because of the sheer complexity and computational cost of upgrading them. All of this makes a large graph increasingly difficult to maintain.

One of the biggest operational problems occur at the ingestion layer. For every new document that needs to be added, a few questions emerge recurrently that need answering.

## Why This Matters

Questions such as the following: Does Sony Corp already exist in the graph? Is the Sony Corp listed in this new document the same entity as Sony Interactive Entertainment already present in the graph? Or do they hold different relationships to our organization, thereby requiring a distinct, new node?

This adds to the ongoing AI race that's captivating the tech world.

## The Bottom Line

In the absence of an effective tool that can narrow the search space, ingestion pipelines are forced to execute expensive global graph searches to scan for variations, which degrade performance and incurs large computational costs. What if there was a scalable, low-cost and fast way to scan thousands of historical documents that have already been ingested into the graph and determine the likely entities and relations before querying the knowledge graph.
