## What's Happening

Listen up: Learn how living goblin sharks recorded near Jarvis Island and in the Tonga Trench extended the species known Central Pacific range and set a new depth record for mackerel sharks.

In 2025, a doctoral student at the University of Hawai‘i at Mānoa sat down with archived video from a 2019 deep-sea expedition, following up on a tip from colleagues. They had mentioned that a strange-looking shark might have drifted past the camera, in a part of the Pacific where it had no business being there. (let that sink in)

Aaron Judah scrolled through the footage until the long, blade-like snout came into frame.

## The Details

There it was, alive and unbothered, a goblin shark far from anywhere it was supposed to live. That clip, paired with a second sighting near one of the deepest places on Earth, has produced the first published observations of a living goblin shark in its natural deep-ocean home.

The study, led his colleagues at UH Mānoa and published in the Journal of Fish Biology , documents two encounters with the shark, one at a seamount near Jarvis Island and another on the slope of the Tonga Trench. Until now, the species had only ever been seen alive after being hooked on a fishing line and hauled to the surface, where it would briefly be observed before dying.

## Why This Matters

“Seeing the most iconic of all the deep-sea sharks alive and looking healthy in its natural habitat is a unique honour,” dropped Judah in a press release . The goblin shark is the sole survivor of a shark family whose lineage runs back nearly 125 million years, which is why it gets called a living fossil. Yet for an animal that famous, no one had ever watched a healthy one go about its life.

This could have implications for future research in this area.

## The Bottom Line

Goblin Shark Sighting Breaks a Deep-Sea Record The Tonga Trench observation, recorded in 2024 by a baited camera on the seafloor during an expedition led -UWA Deep-Sea Research Center, sat over 2,000 feet (nearly 700 meters) deeper than the species was known to venture. According to Judah, that single sighting extends the depth record not just for goblin sharks but for the entire order of mackerel sharks, the group that also includes white sharks, makos, and basking sharks.
