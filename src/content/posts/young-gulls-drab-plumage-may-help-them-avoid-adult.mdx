In American herring gulls, this immature coloring can function as a social signal , helping youngsters avoid aggression from breeding adults, researchers report June 4 in Animal Behavior .

## The Details

Using plastic models painted to resemble gulls of different ages, researchers found that adult gulls were less aggressive toward the gray and brown models of 1-year-old gulls than the bright white, gray and black models of adults. The experiment “works toward answering why so many seabirds have this nonadult plumage retained for so long,” says Gavin Leighton, an evolutionary biologist at Buffalo State University who was not involved in the research.

Researchers used custom-painted plastic birds to test how breeding gulls respond to intruders of different plumage colors near their nests. Liam Taylor Performing the study was no easy task.

## Why This Matters

Breeding colonies of American herring gulls ( Larus smithsonianus ) are no place for the faint of heart. “At these colonies, there’ll be a nest every two to three feet, sometimes. They can be insanely densely packed,” says Molly Hill, an undergraduate at Yale University at the time the research was conducted on Canada’s Kent Island in New Brunswick.
