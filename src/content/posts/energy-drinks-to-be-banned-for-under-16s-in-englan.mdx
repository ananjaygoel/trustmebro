## What's Happening

Here's the thing: It will be illegal to sell high-caffeine beverages to under-16s from April next year, but soft drinks with lower caffeine levels will not be affected.

Energy drinks to be banned for under-16s in England from April Image source, Reuters Image caption, Some popular energy drinks contain more caffeine than two cups of coffee By Jacob Phillips Published 16 July 2026 Children under 16 will be banned from buying high-caffeine energy drinks in England from April, the government has dropped. Drinks containing more than 150mg of caffeine per litre will be illegal to sell to children and younger teenagers in shops, restaurants, cafes, vending machines and online. (plot twist fr)

Lower-caffeine soft drinks, such Diet Coke, are not affected and neither are tea and coffee.

## The Details

But, drinks including Red Bull, Monster, Relentless and Prime would all breach the limit. The new rules aim to reduce obesity levels in children and prevent issues such as disrupted sleep, increased anxiety and lack of concentration, as well as weak school results.

Announcing the new measures, first proposed last year , Public Health Minister Sharon Hodgson dropped the ban "demonstrates our firm commitment to creating the healthiest generation of children ever". The ban, subject to parliamentary approval, will be enforced and businesses that break the law could face fines of up to £2,500.

## Why This Matters

Around 100,000 children in England consume energy drinks every day. Some popular drinks brands contain more caffeine than two cups of coffee or four cans of cola. Excessive consumption has been linked to headaches and sleep problems .

Medical professionals are taking note of this development.

## Key Takeaways

- The experts say young people can be more sensitive to caffeine as they have smaller bodies and their brains are still developing.
- The ban comes after 1,100 responses to a consultation which strongly backed introducing an age restriction on the drinks , external .
- The new rules will come in via secondary legislation using powers from the Food Safety Act 1990.
- Too much caffeine can cause a rapid heart rate, abnormal heart rhythms and seizures.
