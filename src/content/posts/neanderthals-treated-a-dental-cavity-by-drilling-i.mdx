The lower second molar – plagued decay – features tell-tale marks of experienced stone-tool boring, in three stages, down to the pulp. While the procedure would have been excruciating, it probably led to pain relief in the individual, who went on to chew with the tooth, possibly for years, says Kseniya Kolobova at the Russian Academy of Sciences. ## The Details

“We trust the evidence from our microscopes. ” In the Altai mountains of south-western Siberia, Russia – where Neanderthals migrated from Europe about 70,000 years ago – researchers found out a lower molar with a large, irregularly shaped concavity comprising three partially overlapping dips into the entire pulp chamber.

At first, the team thought it was just a tooth that got broken before or after death, says Andrey Krivoshapkin , also at the Russian Academy of Sciences. But as more scientists examined the tooth, more questions arose about how the tooth got deformed – and if it had been done on purpose.

## Why This Matters

Thus far, the oldest evidence of purposeful dental work dates to 14,000 years ago in a Homo sapiens in Italy and involved scratching, not drilling.

Scientists and researchers are watching this development closely.
