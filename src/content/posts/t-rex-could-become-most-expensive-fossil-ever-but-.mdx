## What's Happening

Real talk: A 67 million-year-old Tyrannosaurus rex will go on sale in New York with a pre-sale value of $30m.

Rex could become most expensive fossil ever - but its a ​problem for scientists Published 12 July 2026 Image source, Matthew Sherman By Esme Stallard Senior climate and science reporter In 1997, Sothebys hosted a natural history auction selling wonders of our prehistoric world - but for the first time a dinosaur was on the books. It was a niche event mostly attended s museums looking for specimens to add to their collections. (wild, right?)

The dinosaur in question was a Tyrannosaurus Rex called Sue - she was at some point sold for $8m (£6m) to the Field Museum in Chicago.

## The Details

Nearly 30 years later, on Tuesday, another T. Rex will make an appearance at the annual auction - one of the most complete specimens of this kind ever found.

And this time it is not just scientists who are dinosaur-hunting but also the super-rich. The new specimen, known as Gus, has already been valued at $30m but it could fetch more, possibly even becoming the most expensive dinosaur ever sold.

## Why This Matters

It adds to a growing debate in the natural history world – should specimens of such scientific importance be reserved for museums and their scientists? Or - as auctioneers would argue - should fossil hunters be rewarded for their discovery of dinosaurs took an L to science and saving them from a second extinction? Cassandra Hatton, global head of natural history at Sothebys, knows well the lengths some fossil scientists - palaeontologists - are willing to go to in the search for these creatures.

Scientists and researchers are watching this development closely.
