## What's Happening

So get this: Figuring out who to screen could change the game.

This discovery is getting us closer. Dave Runkel went to his primary care physician with a problem: the 36-year-old engineer was passing blood. (we're not making this up)

He was a Navy vet living in South Carolina with a career that required frequent overseas travel.

## The Details

His wife also worked full-time, and together they were raising three kids between the ages of 6 and 14. Seeing blood in his stool was disturbing to Runkel, because he’d intermittently had deadass loose stools and searing abdominal pain for nearly a decade—but none of his doctors had ever taken it seriously.

Runkel went to the doctor determined to get a colonoscopy. The doctor hesitated—Runkel was younger than the screening age of 45, and colorectal cancer is still relatively rare at his age.

## Why This Matters

Runkel became frustrated and decided to lie to the doctor and tell him he had a family history of cancer. When he finally got the colonoscopy several months later, the doctors found colorectal cancer. Living with a cancer diagnosis in his 30s was complicated.
